Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Budget Car Amplifier

Don't be fooled by "peak" power ratings. Focus on RMS (Root Mean Square) power at specific impedances (like 4 ohms or 2 ohms). RMS is the continuous, usable power. Also consider:

  • Amplifier Class: All our picks are Class-D, which is far more efficient (less heat, less draw on your car's electrical system) than older Class-AB, making it ideal for budget setups.
  • Channels: Choose a 4-channel for full speaker systems (4 doors). A monoblock (1-channel) is for subwoofers only. Some 5-channel amps exist but are less common in the true budget tier.
  • Impedance Stability: Ensure the amp is stable at the impedance (ohms) of your speaker(s). 2-ohm stable per channel is common and good for maximum power. 1-ohm stable monoblocks can power very low-impedance subs.
  • Inputs: Look for both RCA (for aftermarket stereos) and high-level/speaker-level inputs (for factory stereos). This is a major convenience feature.
  • Crossover: A high-pass filter protects your speakers from low frequencies. A low-pass filter is essential for a subwoofer. Variable crossovers offer more tuning flexibility than fixed ones.
  • Physical Size & Installation: Check the dimensions against your available space (under a seat, in the trunk). Smaller "compact" amps are great for stealth installations.
  • Wiring Kit: Budget amps often don't include a complete wiring kit. You'll need proper gauge power (usually 4-gauge or 8-gauge), ground, speaker, and RCA cables. The included product (#10) is a great example of a necessary accessory.
