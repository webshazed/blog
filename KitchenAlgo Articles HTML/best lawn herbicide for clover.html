Clover (Trifolium repens) is a persistent, low-growing perennial that quicklyinvades lawns, forming dense mats that outcompete grass for nutrients, water, and sunlight. While clover fixes nitrogen and can benefit soil health, many homeowners desire a uniform, grass-only lawn for aesthetics and playability. Eliminating established clover requires a targeted post-emergent herbicide that kills the weed without harming your turfgrass. Choosing the wrong product can lead to patchy lawns, wasted money, and potential environmental harm.

Buying Guide: How to Choose the Right Herbicide for Clover

Selecting the best herbicide for clover involves understanding your lawn’s specific situation and the product’s characteristics. Here are the key factors to consider:

  • Selectivity: Choose a selective herbicide (like Scotts Weed & Feed3, Ortho WeedClear, or Southern Ag 2,4-D) if clover is growing amongst desirable grass. These target broadleaf weeds while sparing most grasses. Non-selective herbicides (like Ortho GroundClear or Spectracide) kill all plants they touch and are only for spot treatments on driveways, sidewalks, or before lawn renovation.
  • Active Ingredients: Look for proven broadleaf weed killers. 2,4-D (found in Southern Ag) is a classic, highly effective against clover. Dicamba and MCPP are also common and often combined with 2,4-D for broader spectrum control. Natural options rely on acetic acid (vinegar) or citric acid, which are contact-only and less persistent.
  • Application Method: Consider your equipment and lawn size. Ready-to-spray (hose-end or spray bottle) offers convenience. Concentrates (like Southern Ag) require mixing but are more economical for large areas. Granules (like Scotts Weed & Feed) are spread with a fertilizer spreader and are less prone to drift.
  • Grass Type Compatibility: Always check the label. Some herbicides are safe for cool-season grasses (fescue, Kentucky bluegrass, ryegrass) and warm-season grasses (Bermuda, Zoysia, St. Augustine), while others may injure certain varieties. St. Augustine grass, for example, is sensitive to many common herbicides.
  • Infestation Level: For light, young clover, natural/organic options may suffice. For heavy, established clover, a potent synthetic selective herbicide (like 2,4-D based) is more reliable and may require multiple applications.
  • Safety & Environmental Impact: If you have children, pets, or a vegetable garden, look for lower-toxicity options. Natural Armor and ECO Garden PRO are safer once dried. Synthetic herbicides require strict adherence to safety instructions (PPE, keep off pets/people until dry).
  • Additional Benefits: Some products, like Scotts Weed & Feed3, include fertilizer to nourish your lawn after weed removal. This helps grass recover and compete against future weeds.

Conclusion

Clover doesn’t have to take over your lawn. The best herbicide for your situation depends on the severity of the infestation, your grass type, and your preference for chemical vs. natural solutions.
