Buying Guide: What to Look For in a Budget 4-Channel Amp

Choosing the right amplifier hinges on understanding a few key specifications. Here’s what matters most when shopping on a budget:

  • RMS vs. Peak Power: Ignore the exaggerated "Max" or "Peak" numbers. Focus on RMS (Root Mean Square) power ratings at a specific impedance (e.g., 4 ohms, 2 ohms). RMS represents continuous, usable power. A 400W RMS amp will sound better and be more reliable than a "1000W Max" amp with only 200W RMS.
  • Impedance Stability: Check the ohm rating (e.g., 2-ohms stable, 1-ohm stable). An amp stable at 2 ohms can safely power most 4-ohm speakers. An amp stable at 1 ohm can deliver maximum power to a subwoofer wired to 1 ohm.
  • Class (D vs. A/B): Class D is more efficient, runs cooler, and is smaller—ideal for modern, high-power budget amps. Class A/B is less efficient but often praised for a "warmer," more musical sound signature at lower volumes. For pure power efficiency in a budget amp, Class D is usually the winner.
  • Crossover: A full-range crossover (high-pass for speakers, low-pass for subs) is essential. It allows you to filter frequencies, protecting your speakers and improving sound quality. Ensure it's adjustable.
  • Inputs: Look for both RCA (for aftermarket head units) and high-level ( speaker-level) inputs. High-level inputs are crucial for integrating with factory stereos that lack pre-amp outputs.
  • Physical Size & Installation: Measure your available space. Compact "mini" amps are great for tight spots but may have lower power. Ensure you have adequate ventilation.
