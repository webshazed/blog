Buying Guide: How to Choose the Right Clover Killer for Your Lawn

Selecting the best clover killer depends on your lawn's condition, your experience with herbicides, and your long-term lawn care goals. Here are the critical factors to consider:

  • Selectivity is Paramount: For clover in a lawn you want to keep, choose a selective herbicide containing active ingredients like 2,4-D, dicamba, MCPP, or quinclorac. These target broadleaf weeds while sparing grass. Non-selective herbicides (glyphosate) will kill your grass alongside the clover.
  • Active Ingredients: Look for products with proven broadleaf herbicides. 2,4-D is the most common and effective against clover. Combination products (e.g., 2,4-D + dicamba + MCPP) often provide broader spectrum control. Always check the label for the exact ingredients.
  • Application Form: Ready-to-spray (hose-end or spray bottle) is easiest for beginners. Concentrates require mixing but are more economical for large areas. Granular pre-emergents are spread with a fertilizer spreader and are for prevention only.
  • Coverage and Concentration: Ensure the product size matches your lawn area. Concentrates like Southern Ag offer high coverage per ounce but need proper mixing. Granular products cover large square footages but require watering in.
  • Timing and Weather: Apply selective herbicides when weeds are actively growing (typically spring or fall, temperatures between 60-80°F). Avoid application in extreme heat or rain forecast within 24 hours. Pre-emergents must be applied before weed seeds germinate (early spring).
  • Lawn Type and Safety: Verify the product is safe for your specific grass (e.g., Bermuda, Kentucky bluegrass, St. Augustine). Some herbicides can injure certain grasses. Always follow label instructions regarding waiting periods after seeding or sodding.
  • Integrated Approach: For severe clover problems, combine a post-emergent (to kill existing clover) with a pre-emergent (to prevent new seeds) and proper lawn maintenance (mowing high, proper fertilization, aeration) to outcompete weeds.
