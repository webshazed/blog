Buying Guide: How to Choose the Right Tool

The term "gas-powered pole saw" is somewhat of a misnomer in today’s market. True factory-built gas pole saws are rare, so your choice typically comes down to a gas chainsaw (to which you’d add a pole adapter) or a dedicated electric/battery pole saw. Here’s what to consider:

  • Power Source & Runtime: Gas chainsaws offer unlimited runtime (refuel) and maximum torque for thick, hard wood. Electric pole saws provide consistent power until the battery depletes—ideal for medium-duty pruning. Consider job duration and portability needs.
  • Reach: For high branches, look for pole saws with extension poles (typically 8-15 feet). If using a gas chainsaw with an adapter, ensure the adapter is sturdy and the combined tool isn’t too heavy for overhead work.
  • Bar Length: 6-8 inch bars are common for pole saws, handling branches up to 4-6 inches. Gas chainsaws often have 16-20 inch bars for larger material, but they’re overkill for fine pruning and add weight.
  • Weight & Balance: A lighter tool reduces fatigue. Electric pole saws usually weigh 6-12 lbs., while gas chainsaws start at 10 lbs. and go up. If using overhead, a lighter, well-balanced tool is crucial.
  • Safety Features: Look for chain brakes, automatic oilers, and low-kickback chains. Electric models often have less vibration, which reduces user fatigue and risk.
  • Maintenance: Gas engines require fuel mixing, air filter cleaning, and spark plug checks. Electric tools need only chain sharpening and occasional oiling. Factor in your willingness to maintain a gas engine.
  • Budget: Gas chainsaws and pole adapters can be a premium investment upfront. Electric pole saws often come as kits with batteries and chargers, offering better value for casual users.
